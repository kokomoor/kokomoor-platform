# Cover Letter Style Guide

## Purpose
Write a one-page, high-substance cover letter that makes a clear case for role fit using concrete evidence from the candidate profile.

## Voice and tone
- Confident, respectful, and direct.
- Professional but human. Avoid robotic phrasing.
- Show conviction without overclaiming.
- Write like a peer who respects the reader's time, not a supplicant.
- Vary sentence length. Short declarative sentences between longer ones.

## Structure
1. Intro paragraph
   - State role/company alignment quickly.
   - Establish motivation tied to company mission and job scope.
   - Open with a specific hook, not a stock phrase.
2. Body paragraph(s)
   - Present 2-4 concrete examples tied to priority requirements.
   - Quantify impact where possible.
   - Explain why those examples matter for this role, not just what happened.
   - Each paragraph should advance a distinct argument, not repeat the same one.
3. Closing paragraph
   - Reaffirm fit and motivation with specifics about the company.
   - Close with clear, polite forward-looking language.

## Voice benchmarks
The following excerpts from the candidate's own letters demonstrate the target voice. Match this register and specificity, not the exact structure.

### Benchmark A: Direct, mission-oriented (defense/engineering)
> I'm drawn to the work you do because it sits at the intersection of what I obsess over: machine learning, autonomy, drones, and high-consequence hardware and software systems. This is the category of problems where "pretty good" isn't good enough, and where the best teams ship capability fast.

### Benchmark B: Evidence-dense body paragraph (startup/product)
> The closest parallel in my background is Gauntlet-42, the startup I cofounded. We built Spyglass, an AI-powered automated research engine that uses LLM orchestration, OCR, and NLP extraction in a recursive agentic workflow to replace hours of manual public-records research with minutes of automated work. I designed the process architecture, built the client onboarding and feedback loops, directed pricing and GTM, and managed the team.

### Benchmark C: Company-specific close (strategic)
> What draws me to CFS is the combination of frontier technical challenge and the urgency of execution. The Senior Technical Project Manager role isn't just about tracking timelines, it's about aligning engineering and partner development roadmaps, managing cross-system dependencies, de-risking technical interfaces, and building the collaborative infrastructure needed to deliver at scale. I'm motivated by that kind of complexity.

## Strengths to preserve
- Specific accomplishments with measurable outcomes (numbers, project names, metrics).
- Credible transitions between technical depth and leadership scope.
- Clear mapping from candidate evidence to role requirements.
- First-person voice that sounds human ("I obsess over," "I want to build").

## Weaknesses to avoid
- Generic filler and stock enthusiasm lines ("I am excited to apply," "proven track record").
- Unsupported claims with no evidence anchor.
- Repeating the same claim in different wording across paragraphs.
- Laundry-list style bulleting or fragmented prose.
- Flattery without substance ("your amazing company," "incredible mission").
- AI-tell vocabulary: delve, synergy, tapestry, beacon, paradigm, multifaceted, pivotal, myriad, plethora, unwavering, stellar, cornerstone, empower, unleash.

## Hard constraints
- No em dashes.
- No en dashes in prose.
- Complete sentences with standard business punctuation.
- No placeholders.
- Keep to one page while using meaningful detail (target 300-420 words).
